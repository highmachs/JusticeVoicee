
*** LAW: PROTECTION OF WOMEN FROM DOMESTIC VIOLENCE ACT (PWDVA), 2005 ***

SECTION 3: Definition of Domestic Violence
Domestic violence includes any act, omission, or commission that harms, injures, or endangers the health, safety, life, limb, or well-being of the aggrieved woman. It covers:
(a) Physical abuse: hitting, slapping, kicking, biting, pushing, shoving, or any act causing bodily pain or injury.
(b) Sexual abuse: any conduct of a sexual nature that abuses, humiliates, or violates the dignity of the woman, including marital rape (forced sexual intercourse by husband).
(c) Verbal and emotional abuse: insults, ridicule, humiliation, name calling, constant criticism, accusations of infidelity, threats of injury, threats to take away children, preventing the woman from taking a job.
(d) Economic abuse: not providing money for food, clothing, medicine, or other necessities. Depriving the woman of her stridhan (her own property/gifts). Not paying rent or mortgage. Alienation of assets without consent.

SECTION 4: Information given to Protection Officer, service provider or Magistrate
Any person who has reason to believe that domestic violence has been, is being, or is likely to be committed, may inform the Protection Officer.

SECTION 5: Duties of Police Officers, Service Providers
The police officer must inform the aggrieved woman of her right to: make an application for relief, obtain services of service providers, file a complaint under Section 498A of IPC (now BNS Section 85), and free legal aid.

SECTION 12: Application to Magistrate
An aggrieved woman, Protection Officer, or any person on behalf of the aggrieved woman can present an application to the Magistrate seeking relief. The Magistrate must fix the first hearing within 3 days and dispose of the case within 60 days.

SECTION 17: Right to Reside in Shared Household
Every woman in a domestic relationship has the right to reside in the shared household, whether or not she has any title or right in it. The respondent cannot evict or exclude the aggrieved woman from the shared household.

SECTION 18: Protection Orders
The Magistrate can pass protection orders prohibiting the respondent from:
(a) committing any act of domestic violence
(b) aiding or abetting domestic violence
(c) entering the aggrieved woman's workplace or school
(d) attempting to communicate with the aggrieved woman
(e) alienating any assets, bank accounts, or stridhan

SECTION 19: Residence Orders
The Magistrate can direct the respondent to remove himself from the shared household, restrain the respondent from entering any portion of the shared household, or direct the respondent to secure alternate accommodation for the aggrieved woman.

SECTION 20: Monetary Relief
The Magistrate can direct the respondent to pay monetary relief to meet expenses including:
(a) loss of earnings
(b) medical expenses
(c) loss caused by destruction or damage of property
(d) maintenance for the aggrieved woman and her children

SECTION 21: Custody Orders
The Magistrate can grant temporary custody of any child or children to the aggrieved woman. The respondent may be granted visitation rights if it is in the interest of the child.

SECTION 22: Compensation Orders
The Magistrate can direct payment of compensation and damages for injuries, including mental torture and emotional distress caused by domestic violence.

SECTION 23: Emergency Orders (Ex-Parte)
If the Magistrate is satisfied that the application discloses domestic violence or there is imminent danger, the Magistrate can pass an interim ex-parte order on the basis of the affidavit filed, without hearing the respondent.

SECTION 31: Breach of Protection Order is a Crime
Any breach of a protection order is a cognizable and non-bailable offence, punishable with imprisonment up to one year or fine up to Rs. 20,000 or both.


*** LAW: BHARATIYA NYAYA SANHITA (BNS), 2023 ***
REPLACES: Indian Penal Code (IPC), 1860
EFFECTIVE FROM: July 1, 2024

SECTION 85: Cruelty by Husband or Relatives (Formerly IPC 498A)
Whoever, being the husband or the relative of the husband of a woman, subjects such woman to cruelty shall be punished with imprisonment for a term which may extend to three years and shall also be liable to fine. Cruelty means any wilful conduct which is of such a nature as is likely to drive the woman to commit suicide or to cause grave injury or danger to life, limb, or health (whether mental or physical) of the woman. It also includes harassment for dowry.

SECTION 86: Dowry Death (Formerly IPC 304B)
Where the death of a woman is caused by burns, bodily injury, or occurs otherwise than under normal circumstances within seven years of her marriage, and it is shown that soon before her death she was subjected to cruelty or harassment by her husband or any relative for dowry, such death shall be called dowry death. Punishment: imprisonment of not less than seven years, extending to life imprisonment.

SECTION 64: Punishment for Rape (Formerly IPC 376)
A man is said to commit rape who has sexual intercourse with a woman against her will, without her consent, by coercion, when she is intoxicated, or when she is unable to communicate consent. Punishment: Rigorous imprisonment of not less than 10 years, which may extend to life imprisonment, and fine. For gang rape: not less than 20 years rigorous imprisonment, extending to life imprisonment.

SECTION 65: Rape of a Girl Under 16
Punishment: not less than 20 years rigorous imprisonment, extending to life imprisonment (for the remainder of natural life).

SECTION 66: Rape of a Girl Under 12
Punishment: not less than 20 years rigorous imprisonment, extending to life imprisonment or death.

SECTION 67: Rape by Husband during Separation
If a man has sexual intercourse with his own wife, who is living separately under a decree of separation or under any custom or usage, without her consent, he shall be punished with imprisonment of not less than 2 years, extending to 7 years, and fine.

SECTION 69: Sexual Intercourse by Deceitful Means
Whoever, by deceitful means or by making a false promise to marry without any intention of fulfilling it, has sexual intercourse with a woman, not amounting to rape, shall be punished with imprisonment extending to 10 years and fine.

SECTION 74: Assault or Use of Criminal Force on Woman with Intent to Outrage Modesty (Groping, Molestation)
Punishment: imprisonment of not less than 1 year, extending to 5 years, and fine.

SECTION 75: Sexual Harassment (Eve-Teasing)
Any man who makes directly physically unwelcome contact and advances, or demands or requests for sexual favours, or makes sexually coloured remarks, or forcibly shows pornography, or follows a woman and contacts or attempts to contact for fostering personal interaction repeatedly despite clear indication of disinterest, or monitors a woman using the internet or electronic communication shall be punished with imprisonment up to 3 years and fine.

SECTION 76: Assault or Use of Criminal Force to Disrobe a Woman
Whoever assaults or uses criminal force to any woman with the intent of disrobing her or compelling her to be naked, shall be punished with imprisonment of not less than 3 years extending to 7 years, and fine.

SECTION 77: Voyeurism
Watching or capturing the image of a woman engaging in a private act without her consent. Punishment: 1-3 years imprisonment and fine for first conviction; 3-7 years and fine for second conviction.

SECTION 78: Stalking
Repeatedly following a woman, contacting or attempting to contact her despite her clear indication of disinterest, or monitoring her use of the internet. Punishment: up to 3 years for first conviction and up to 5 years for second conviction, and fine.

SECTION 79: Word, Gesture or Act Intended to Insult the Modesty of a Woman
Whoever, intending to insult the modesty of any woman, utters any word, makes any sound or gesture, or exhibits any object intending that such word or sound shall be heard, or that such gesture or object shall be seen by such woman, shall be punished with imprisonment up to 3 years and fine.

SECTION 95-96: Kidnapping and Abduction of Woman (Formerly IPC 366)
Whoever kidnaps or abducts any woman with intent that she may be compelled to marry against her will, or be forced to have sexual intercourse, shall be punished with imprisonment up to 10 years and fine.

SECTION 124: Acid Attack
Causing permanent or partial damage, deformity, burns, maiming, disfigurement, disability, or grievous hurt by throwing or administering acid. Punishment: imprisonment of not less than 10 years, extending to life, and fine (paid to the victim for medical treatment).


*** LAW: BHARATIYA NAGARIK SURAKSHA SANHITA (BNSS), 2023 ***
REPLACES: Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC), 1973
EFFECTIVE FROM: July 1, 2024

SECTION 144: Order for Maintenance of Wives, Children and Parents (Formerly CrPC 125)
If any person having sufficient means neglects or refuses to maintain his wife (unable to maintain herself), legitimate or illegitimate minor child, or aged or infirm parents, a Magistrate may order monthly maintenance not exceeding Rs. 500 per month. The wife is entitled to maintenance even if she is living separately if the husband has married again, or keeps a mistress, or has been convicted of cruelty.
Important: The application for maintenance must be disposed of within 60 days from the date of notice.

SECTION 173: Zero FIR
A complaint can be registered at any police station regardless of the area where the offence was committed. The FIR will then be transferred to the appropriate police station. This means women do not need to go to a specific police station; any police station must accept their complaint.

SECTION 176: Compulsory Registration of FIR in Crimes Against Women
The police are bound to register an FIR for cognizable offences. If the police refuse, the complainant can send the complaint by post to the Superintendent of Police, or approach the Magistrate under Section 175.

SECTION 193: Arrest of Woman — Special Provisions
No woman shall be arrested after sunset and before sunrise except in exceptional circumstances, and that too by a woman police officer with the prior permission of the Judicial Magistrate.

IMPORTANT: Under the new law (BNSS 2023), women victims of sexual offences have the right to give their statement through audio-video electronic means and to a woman police officer or woman magistrate.


*** LAW: DOWRY PROHIBITION ACT, 1961 ***

SECTION 2: Definition of Dowry
Dowry means any property or valuable security given or agreed to be given either directly or indirectly: (a) by one party to a marriage to the other party, (b) by parents of either party, (c) by any other person, to either party or any other person, at or before or any time after the marriage in connection with the marriage.

SECTION 3: Penalty for Giving or Taking Dowry
Giving or taking dowry is punishable with imprisonment for not less than 5 years and a fine of not less than Rs. 15,000 or the value of the dowry, whichever is more.

SECTION 4: Penalty for Demanding Dowry
Demanding dowry, directly or indirectly, from the parents or guardians of a bride or bridegroom is punishable with imprisonment for 6 months to 2 years and fine up to Rs. 10,000.

SECTION 6: Dowry to be for the Benefit of the Woman
Where any dowry is received by any person other than the woman, it shall be transferred to the woman within 3 months. If not transferred, imprisonment up to 2 years and fine.

SECTION 8A: Burden of Proof
In any prosecution for taking or abetting the taking of dowry, the burden of proving that no dowry was demanded lies on the person accused, not the victim.


*** LAW: SEXUAL HARASSMENT OF WOMEN AT WORKPLACE (POSH) ACT, 2013 ***

SECTION 2: Definition of Sexual Harassment
Sexual harassment includes any one or more of the following unwelcome acts: (i) physical contact and advances, (ii) a demand or request for sexual favours, (iii) making sexually coloured remarks, (iv) showing pornography, (v) any other unwelcome physical, verbal or non-verbal conduct of sexual nature. It also includes implied or explicit promise of preferential treatment, implied or explicit threat of detrimental treatment, interference with work, or creating a hostile or offensive work environment.

SECTION 3: Every woman shall be protected from sexual harassment at any workplace.

SECTION 4: Internal Complaints Committee (ICC)
Every employer with 10 or more employees must constitute an ICC. The ICC must be headed by a senior woman employee, have at least 2 members committed to women's causes, and one external member from an NGO. At least half the members must be women.

SECTION 6: Local Complaints Committee (LCC)
For workplaces with fewer than 10 employees or for complaints against the employer, a Local Complaints Committee is constituted at the district level.

SECTION 9: Filing a Complaint
An aggrieved woman can file a written complaint within 3 months of the incident (extendable by 3 months). The complaint can also be filed by her legal heir in case of her death or incapacity.

SECTION 11: Inquiry
The ICC must complete its inquiry within 90 days. During the inquiry, the ICC can recommend transfer of the complainant or the respondent, or grant leave up to 3 months to the complainant.

SECTION 13: Action After Inquiry
If sexual harassment is proved, the ICC recommends: (a) disciplinary action against the respondent as per service rules, (b) deduction from salary or wages to pay compensation to the complainant.

SECTION 14: Punishment for False Complaint
Action can be taken against false or malicious complaints, but mere inability to prove does not mean the complaint is false.

SECTION 19: Duties of Employer
Every employer must: display information about the POSH Act, organize awareness programs, provide assistance to the complainant in filing complaints, and treat sexual harassment as misconduct under service rules.


*** LAW: MEDICAL TERMINATION OF PREGNANCY (MTP) ACT, 2021 ***

Gestation Limits:
Up to 20 weeks: Opinion of 1 Registered Medical Practitioner (RMP) required.
20 to 24 weeks: Opinion of 2 RMPs required. Extended limit applies to: survivors of rape or incest, minors, change of marital status during pregnancy, women with physical or mental disabilities, cases of fetal malformation, women in humanitarian settings.
Beyond 24 weeks: Only for substantial fetal abnormalities, diagnosed by a State Medical Board.

Privacy (Section 5A): The name and other particulars of a woman whose pregnancy has been terminated SHALL NOT be revealed except to a person authorized by law.

Unmarried women are also entitled to MTP up to 24 weeks under the 2021 amendment.
No requirement of husband's consent. The woman's own consent is sufficient.


*** LAW: MATERNITY BENEFIT ACT, 1961 (Amended 2017) ***

Leave Entitlement:
First two children: 26 weeks paid maternity leave (8 weeks before expected delivery, 18 weeks after).
Third child onwards: 12 weeks paid leave.
Commissioning mothers (surrogacy): 12 weeks from the date the child is handed over.
Adoptive mothers (child below 3 months): 12 weeks from the date of adoption.

Work from Home: After the 26-week leave, the employer may allow the woman to work from home if the nature of work allows it. The terms are mutually agreed.

Creche Facility: Every establishment with 50 or more employees must have a creche within a prescribed distance. The woman is allowed 4 visits to the creche during the day.

Job Security: It is unlawful for an employer to dismiss, discharge, or reduce the pay of a woman during maternity leave. Any such dismissal is void.

Nursing Breaks: After returning to work, nursing mothers are entitled to two nursing breaks of 15 minutes each, in addition to regular intervals for rest, until the child is 15 months old.


*** LAW: EQUAL REMUNERATION ACT, 1976 (Now Part of Code on Wages, 2019) ***

No employer shall pay to any worker employed by him, remuneration at rates less favorable than those at which remuneration is paid by him to workers of the opposite sex for performing same work or work of a similar nature. Discrimination in recruitment of women for same or similar work is also prohibited. Any violation is punishable with fine of up to Rs. 10,000 or imprisonment up to 3 months.


*** LAW: HINDU SUCCESSION ACT, 1956 (Amended 2005) ***

Section 6 (Amended): Daughters have equal coparcenary rights as sons in Hindu joint family property. This amendment is retrospective, meaning daughters who were alive on September 9, 2005 get equal rights regardless of when they were born.

Section 14: Any property possessed by a Hindu female, whether acquired before or after the commencement of this Act, shall be held by her as full owner and not as a limited owner.

Section 15-16: When a Hindu woman dies intestate (without a will), her property goes to: firstly children and husband, then heirs of husband, then mother and father, then heirs of father, then heirs of mother.

A married daughter has equal right to her father's self-acquired property.
A woman can make a will for her self-acquired property.


*** LAW: MUSLIM WOMEN (PROTECTION OF RIGHTS ON MARRIAGE) ACT, 2019 ***

Section 3: Any pronouncement of talaq by a Muslim husband upon his wife, by words, either spoken or written or in electronic form or in any other manner whatsoever, shall be void and illegal. (This makes Triple Talaq a criminal offence.)

Section 4: Punishment for pronouncing Triple Talaq: imprisonment for a term which may extend to 3 years and fine.

Section 5: A Muslim woman is entitled to receive subsistence allowance from her husband for herself and her dependent children.

Section 6: A Muslim woman is entitled to the custody of her minor children in case of pronouncement of talaq by her husband.


*** LAW: PROHIBITION OF CHILD MARRIAGE ACT, 2006 ***

Section 2: A child marriage is a marriage where either the bride is below 18 years or the groom is below 21 years.

Section 3: A child marriage is voidable at the option of the contracting party who was a child at the time of marriage. The petition must be filed within 2 years of attaining majority.

Section 9: Punishment for male adult marrying a child: rigorous imprisonment up to 2 years or fine up to Rs. 1 lakh or both.

Section 10: Punishment for solemnizing a child marriage: rigorous imprisonment up to 2 years and fine up to Rs. 1 lakh.

Section 11: Punishment for directing or promoting a child marriage: rigorous imprisonment up to 2 years and fine up to Rs. 1 lakh.


*** LAW: IMMORAL TRAFFIC (PREVENTION) ACT, 1956 ***

Section 3: Punishment for keeping a brothel or allowing premises to be used as a brothel: imprisonment of not less than 1 year extending to 3 years, and fine up to Rs. 2,000.

Section 4: Living on the earnings of prostitution: imprisonment up to 2 years or fine up to Rs. 1,000.

Section 5: Procuring, inducing, or taking a woman for the sake of prostitution: imprisonment of not less than 3 years extending to 7 years, and fine. If the victim is a child, imprisonment of not less than 7 years extending to 14 years.

Section 6: Detaining a woman in premises where prostitution is carried on: imprisonment of not less than 7 years extending to 14 years.

Important: A woman found in a brothel is NOT treated as an offender but as a victim. The law punishes traffickers, pimps, and brothel keepers, not the women.


*** LAW: PROTECTION OF CHILDREN FROM SEXUAL OFFENCES (POCSO) ACT, 2012 ***

This law protects all children (boys and girls) below 18 years from sexual abuse.

Section 3-4: Penetrative sexual assault: imprisonment of not less than 10 years, extending to life. If by a person in authority (teacher, doctor, police, relative), minimum 10 years extending to life.

Section 5-6: Aggravated penetrative sexual assault (by police, armed forces, public servant, relative, gang rape, use of acid, resulting in pregnancy): imprisonment of not less than 20 years extending to life, or death.

Section 7-8: Sexual assault (touching): imprisonment of not less than 3 years, extending to 5 years, and fine.

Section 11-12: Sexual harassment of a child (showing pornography, making sexual gestures, following): imprisonment up to 3 years and fine.

Section 19: Mandatory reporting. Any person who has knowledge that an offence has been committed must report it to the police. Failure to report: imprisonment up to 6 months or fine.

Section 33: Special Courts shall complete trial within 1 year.

Section 35: Presumption of guilt — the burden of proof is on the accused, not the child victim.

Section 36: The child victim shall not be called to court repeatedly. Their statement through video can be used as evidence.


*** LAW: INDECENT REPRESENTATION OF WOMEN (PROHIBITION) ACT, 1986 ***

Section 3: No person shall publish or cause to be published any advertisement which contains indecent representation of women in any form. This includes depiction of the figure of a woman that is derogatory or demeaning.

Section 4: No person shall produce, sell, distribute, or circulate any book, pamphlet, picture, writing, etc. containing indecent representation of women.

Section 6: Punishment: first offence imprisonment up to 2 years and fine up to Rs. 2,000; second offence imprisonment of not less than 6 months extending to 5 years and fine of Rs. 10,000 to Rs. 1,00,000.


*** LAW: NATIONAL COMMISSION FOR WOMEN ACT, 1990 ***

The National Commission for Women (NCW) was established to:
(a) Review safeguards provided for women under the Constitution and other laws
(b) Recommend remedial legislative measures
(c) Look into complaints and take suo motu notice of matters relating to deprivation of women's rights
(d) Inspect jails, remand homes, and institutions for women

*** LAW: INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY ACT, 2000 (Amended 2008) — CYBER CRIMES AGAINST WOMEN ***

SECTION 66C: Identity Theft — Using someone's electronic signature, password, or unique identification feature. Punishment: imprisonment up to 3 years and fine up to Rs. 1 lakh.

SECTION 66D: Cheating by Personation using computer resource (catfishing, fake profiles). Punishment: imprisonment up to 3 years and fine up to Rs. 1 lakh.

SECTION 66E: Violation of Privacy — Capturing, publishing, or transmitting images of a person's private area without consent. Punishment: imprisonment up to 3 years or fine up to Rs. 2 lakh.

SECTION 67: Publishing or transmitting obscene material in electronic form. Punishment: first offence imprisonment up to 3 years and fine up to Rs. 5 lakh.

SECTION 67A: Publishing or transmitting sexually explicit content. Punishment: first offence imprisonment up to 5 years and fine up to Rs. 10 lakh.

SECTION 67B: Publishing or transmitting material depicting children in sexually explicit act. Punishment: first offence imprisonment up to 5 years and fine up to Rs. 10 lakh.

Revenge Porn / Non-Consensual Intimate Images: Covered under BNS Section 77 (voyeurism) and IT Act Section 66E. Also violates right to privacy under Article 21 of the Constitution.

Cyber Stalking: Covered under BNS Section 78 and IT Act Section 66D. Sending repeated offensive emails, messages, or friend requests despite refusal is punishable.

Report cyber crimes: cybercrime.gov.in or call National Cyber Crime Helpline 1930.


*** LAW: PRE-CONCEPTION AND PRE-NATAL DIAGNOSTIC TECHNIQUES (PCPNDT) ACT, 1994 ***

This Act prohibits sex-selective abortion and sex determination of the fetus.

Section 5: No person conducting pre-natal diagnostic procedures shall communicate the sex of the fetus to the pregnant woman or her relatives.

Section 23: Punishment for determination of sex: imprisonment up to 3 years and fine up to Rs. 10,000 for first offence; imprisonment up to 5 years and fine up to Rs. 50,000 for subsequent offence.

Medical practitioners found guilty face suspension of their registration for 5 years (first offence) or permanent removal (second offence).


*** LAW: LEGAL SERVICES AUTHORITIES ACT, 1987 ***

Every woman is entitled to FREE legal aid irrespective of her income. This includes:
(a) Free representation by a lawyer in any court
(b) Free legal advice
(c) Free mediation and conciliation services

To get free legal aid, approach:
District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) at the district court
State Legal Services Authority (SLSA) at the High Court
National Legal Services Authority (NALSA) — Helpline: 15100

No documents or proof of income are required for women to avail free legal aid.


*** CONSTITUTIONAL PROTECTIONS FOR WOMEN ***

Article 14: Equality before law. The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law.
Article 15(1): Prohibition of discrimination on grounds of sex.
Article 15(3): The State may make special provisions for women and children (enables protective laws).
Article 16: Equality of opportunity in public employment regardless of sex.
Article 21: Right to life and personal liberty with dignity. Includes right to live free from violence, right to privacy, right to shelter.
Article 23: Prohibition of human trafficking and forced labor.
Article 39(a): Right to adequate means of livelihood.
Article 39(d): Equal pay for equal work for both men and women.
Article 42: Just and humane conditions of work and maternity relief.
Article 51A(e): Fundamental duty to renounce practices derogatory to the dignity of women.


*** IMPORTANT SUPREME COURT DECISIONS ***

Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997): Established guidelines for prevention of sexual harassment at workplace, which later became the POSH Act 2013.

Lata Singh v. State of UP (2006): A woman of marriageable age has the right to marry anyone of her choice. Inter-caste or inter-religion marriages cannot be stopped by families or communities.

Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017): Triple talaq declared unconstitutional and void. Led to the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019.

Joseph Shine v. Union of India (2018): Adultery is decriminalized. Section 497 IPC struck down as unconstitutional because it treated women as property of their husbands.

Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018): Section 377 partially struck down. Right to sexual autonomy recognized.

Indian Young Lawyers Association v. State of Kerala (2018) (Sabarimala case): Women of all ages have the right to enter religious places of worship.
