
*** LAW: HINDU MARRIAGE ACT, 1955 ***

SECTION 5: Conditions for Valid Marriage
(i) Neither party has a living spouse at the time of marriage (monogamy)
(ii) Neither party is incapable of giving valid consent due to unsoundness of mind
(iii) The bridegroom must be at least 21 years old and the bride at least 18 years old
(iv) The parties are not within degrees of prohibited relationship

SECTION 9: Restitution of Conjugal Rights
When either husband or wife has withdrawn from the other without reasonable excuse, the aggrieved party may apply for restitution. However, the wife can refuse restitution on grounds of cruelty, desertion, or other valid reasons.

SECTION 13: Grounds for Divorce
Either spouse can seek divorce on grounds of: (a) adultery, (b) cruelty, (c) desertion for at least 2 years, (d) conversion to another religion, (e) unsoundness of mind, (f) venereal disease, (g) renunciation of the world, (h) not being heard of as alive for 7 years.

SECTION 13B: Divorce by Mutual Consent
Both parties must have been living separately for at least 1 year. Both must agree that they cannot live together. The court grants divorce after a cooling-off period of 6 months (Supreme Court can waive this). UPDATE: Supreme Court in Amardeep Singh v. Harveen Kaur (2017) held the 6-month cooling period can be waived.

SECTION 24: Maintenance Pendente Lite (During Divorce)
Either spouse who has no independent income sufficient for support can claim maintenance during divorce proceedings. The court can order interim maintenance and litigation expenses.

SECTION 25: Permanent Alimony and Maintenance
At the time of or after granting divorce, the court can order the husband (or wife, if she has sufficient means) to pay permanent alimony. The amount depends on the respondent's income, the applicant's own income, and the standard of living.

SECTION 26: Custody of Children
In any matrimonial proceeding, the court may pass interim orders for the custody, maintenance, and education of minor children. The welfare of the child is the paramount consideration.

SECTION 27: Disposal of Property
The court may make orders for the settlement of property for the benefit of the woman or the children.


*** LAW: SPECIAL MARRIAGE ACT, 1954 ***

This Act enables inter-caste, inter-religion, and civil marriages without converting to another religion.

SECTION 4: Conditions for Marriage
Both parties must be of sound mind, the male must be at least 21 and the female at least 18, and neither should have a living spouse.

SECTION 5: Notice of Intended Marriage
A 30-day notice is required. However, objections based on caste, religion, or community are not valid grounds to stop the marriage.

SECTION 27: Grounds for Divorce (same as Hindu Marriage Act)
Includes cruelty, desertion, adultery, unsoundness of mind, venereal disease, and non-communication for 7 years.

SECTION 36-37: Alimony and Maintenance
The wife is entitled to maintenance during and after divorce proceedings; the court decides the amount based on the husband's income and the wife's needs.


*** LAW: INDIAN DIVORCE ACT, 1869 (For Christians) ***

SECTION 10: Grounds for Dissolution of Marriage
A wife can seek divorce on grounds of: adultery coupled with cruelty or desertion for 2 years, conversion, unsoundness of mind for 2 years, venereal disease for 2 years, cruelty, and desertion for 2 years or more.

SECTION 36-37: Alimony
The wife is entitled to alimony not exceeding one-fifth of the husband's average net income for the preceding three years.


*** LAW: DISSOLUTION OF MUSLIM MARRIAGES ACT, 1939 ***

SECTION 2: Grounds for Decree of Dissolution
A Muslim woman can obtain divorce (called khula or faskh) on the following grounds:
(a) Whereabouts of husband unknown for 4 years
(b) Husband has failed to provide maintenance for 2 years
(c) Husband sentenced to imprisonment of 7 years or more
(d) Husband has failed to perform marital obligations for 3 years
(e) Husband was impotent at the time of marriage and continues to be so
(f) Husband has been insane for 2 years or is suffering from leprosy or venereal disease
(g) The woman was married before age 15 and repudiates the marriage before attaining 18 (option of puberty)
(h) Husband treats her with cruelty — cruelty includes habitual assault, association with women of evil repute, forcing her to lead an immoral life, disposing of her property, obstructing her religious practice, and unequal treatment among multiple wives.


*** LAW: HINDU ADOPTIONS AND MAINTENANCE ACT, 1956 ***

SECTION 18: Maintenance of Wife
A Hindu wife is entitled to be maintained by her husband during her lifetime. She can live separately and still claim maintenance if:
(a) Husband is guilty of desertion
(b) Husband has treated her with cruelty
(c) Husband is suffering from a virulent form of leprosy
(d) Husband has any other wife living
(e) Husband keeps a concubine in the house or regularly commits adultery
(f) Husband has converted to another religion
(g) Any other reasonable cause

SECTION 19: Maintenance of Widowed Daughter-in-Law
A Hindu widow who is unable to maintain herself from her own earnings or other property is entitled to maintenance from her father-in-law's estate or from the husband's coparcenary property.

SECTION 20: Maintenance of Children and Aged Parents
A Hindu is bound to maintain his or her legitimate and illegitimate children and aged or infirm parents who are unable to maintain themselves.


*** LAW: HINDU MINORITY AND GUARDIANSHIP ACT, 1956 ***

SECTION 6: Natural Guardian
The natural guardian of a Hindu minor: for a boy or an unmarried girl, the father, and after him, the mother. For an illegitimate boy or girl, the mother, and after her, the father. For a married girl, the husband.

Supreme Court update: Githa Hariharan v. Reserve Bank of India (1999) — The mother can be the natural guardian even during the father's lifetime. The word "after" does not necessarily mean "after the death of" but can mean "in the absence of."

SECTION 13: Welfare of Minor is Paramount
In appointing or declaring any person as guardian, the court shall be guided by what would be in the welfare of the minor.


*** LAW: CRIMINAL LAW (AMENDMENT) ACT, 2013 — THE NIRBHAYA ACT ***

This landmark legislation was enacted after the 2012 Delhi gang rape case. Key provisions:

New offences added to IPC (now in BNS 2023):
(a) Acid Attack (BNS Section 124): Minimum 10 years imprisonment extending to life
(b) Sexual Harassment (BNS Section 75): Up to 3 years imprisonment
(c) Voyeurism (BNS Section 77): 1-3 years for first offence, 3-7 years for repeat
(d) Stalking (BNS Section 78): Up to 3 years for first offence, up to 5 years for repeat
(e) Disrobing a woman (BNS Section 76): 3-7 years imprisonment

Enhanced punishments:
(a) Gang rape: 20 years to life imprisonment
(b) Rape causing death or vegetative state: 20 years to life or death penalty
(c) Repeat offenders of rape: life imprisonment or death
(d) Rape of girl under 12: 20 years to life or death
(e) Rape of girl under 16: 20 years to life

Other protections:
(a) Victim's statement can be recorded at her residence by a woman police officer
(b) Medical examination of rape victim must be done by a female doctor if available
(c) Character of the victim is irrelevant and shall not be brought into evidence
(d) Consent: absence of physical resistance does not equal consent
(e) Two-finger test on rape survivors is banned (Supreme Court)


*** LAW: SCHEDULED CASTES AND SCHEDULED TRIBES (PREVENTION OF ATROCITIES) ACT, 1989 ***

Special provisions for SC/ST women:

SECTION 3(1)(w): Assault or use of force on any woman belonging to SC/ST with intent to dishonour her: imprisonment 6 months to 5 years and fine.

SECTION 3(1)(xi): Assaults or uses criminal force against a SC/ST woman with intent to disrobe or forces her to be naked in public: imprisonment 3-7 years and fine.

SECTION 3(2)(v): Committing rape on any SC/ST woman: imprisonment of not less than 10 years extending to life and fine.

Key features:
All offences are cognizable and non-bailable.
Special courts are designated for speedy trial.
The government provides legal aid and travel expenses to victims.
Compensation ranging from Rs. 85,000 to Rs. 8,25,000 is provided to victims.
An SC/ST woman's testimony has special evidentiary value and the burden of proof shifts to the accused.


*** LAW: COMMISSION OF SATI (PREVENTION) ACT, 1987 ***

SECTION 3: Attempt to commit sati is punishable with imprisonment up to 1 year or fine or both.
SECTION 4: Abetment of sati is punishable with death or life imprisonment.
SECTION 5: Glorification of sati (building temples, conducting ceremonies) is punishable with imprisonment of 1-7 years and fine.

The Act makes it the duty of police to take measures to prevent sati and provides for Special Courts for trial.


*** LAW: FACTORIES ACT, 1948 — PROVISIONS FOR WOMEN ***

SECTION 27: No woman worker shall be required to lift weights exceeding the prescribed maximum.
SECTION 19: Separate toilet facilities must be provided for women workers.
SECTION 48: Creche facility must be provided in factories with 30 or more women workers.
SECTION 66: No woman shall be required to work between 7 PM and 6 AM (some states have relaxed this with safety conditions).
SECTION 79: Women are entitled to maternity leave and rest.

Women cannot be employed in dangerous operations without adequate safety measures and medical examination.


*** LAW: PLANTATION LABOUR ACT, 1951 ***

SECTION 12: Provision of creches in plantations with 50 or more women workers.
SECTION 18: Separate toilet facilities for women.
SECTION 32: Maternity benefit of 12 weeks with average daily wage.
Women working on plantations are entitled to housing, medical facilities, and education for their children.


*** LAW: CODE ON WAGES, 2019 ***

SECTION 3: No employer shall pay less wages to a woman for work of the same or similar nature.
SECTION 5: No gender discrimination in matters of recruitment for similar work.
This consolidates and replaces the Equal Remuneration Act, 1976.


*** LAW: CODE ON SOCIAL SECURITY, 2020 ***

Chapter VI: Maternity Benefit
SECTION 60: Every woman shall be entitled to maternity benefit for 26 weeks (for first two children) and 12 weeks (for subsequent children).
SECTION 62: No employer shall knowingly employ a woman during 6 weeks immediately following her delivery or miscarriage.
SECTION 65: Nursing breaks: every woman who returns to work after delivery shall be allowed two nursing breaks of prescribed duration until the child attains 15 months of age.
SECTION 67: No dismissal during maternity leave. Any notice of discharge given without sufficient cause within 3 months before or 3 months after delivery is unlawful.


*** LAW: NATIONAL FOOD SECURITY ACT, 2013 ***

SECTION 4: Maternity Benefit
Every pregnant and lactating mother is entitled to: (a) meals free of charge during pregnancy and 6 months after childbirth through the local Anganwadi centre, and (b) maternity benefit of not less than Rs. 6,000 in installments.

SECTION 12: The eldest woman in the household (18 years or above) shall be the "head of household" for the purpose of issuing ration cards.


*** LAW: RIGHT OF CHILDREN TO FREE AND COMPULSORY EDUCATION ACT, 2009 ***

SECTION 3: Every child (including girls) between 6 and 14 years has the right to free and compulsory education in a neighbourhood school.
SECTION 14: No child shall be denied admission for lack of age proof or birth certificate.
SECTION 16: No child shall be held back or expelled from school until completion of elementary education.

Special provision: No girl child can be denied admission in any school on grounds of gender.


*** LAW: JUVENILE JUSTICE (CARE AND PROTECTION OF CHILDREN) ACT, 2015 ***

Protections for girls in conflict with the law and girls in need of care and protection:
SECTION 75: Cruelty to a child by person having charge: imprisonment up to 3 years or fine of Rs. 1 lakh or both.
SECTION 76: Employing a child for begging: imprisonment up to 5 years and fine of Rs. 1 lakh.
SECTION 78: Using a child for vending or selling alcohol or drugs: imprisonment up to 7 years.
SECTION 82: Child Welfare Committees are established in every district for care and protection of children.
All children in institutional care must have access to education, healthcare, nutrition, and recreation.


*** LAW: MAINTENANCE AND WELFARE OF PARENTS AND SENIOR CITIZENS ACT, 2007 ***

SECTION 4: A senior citizen (including elderly women) who is unable to maintain herself from her own earning or property, can apply for maintenance from her children or grandchildren.
SECTION 5: The maximum maintenance amount is now Rs. 10,000 per month (can be revised by states).
SECTION 23: Property transfer made by a senior citizen under coercion or undue influence can be declared void.

Elderly women are specially protected under this Act. If children or relatives abandon elderly parents, they face imprisonment up to 3 months or fine up to Rs. 5,000.


*** LAW: NARCOTIC DRUGS AND PSYCHOTROPIC SUBSTANCES ACT, 1985 ***

SECTION 71: Women who are drug addicts are entitled to treatment, rehabilitation, and social reintegration at de-addiction centres. No woman shall be denied treatment on account of inability to pay.

Women drug users who are pregnant are entitled to pre-natal and post-natal care and access to maternity services during rehabilitation.


*** LAW: BHARATIYA SAKSHYA ADHINIYAM (BSA), 2023 ***
REPLACES: Indian Evidence Act, 1872
EFFECTIVE FROM: July 1, 2024

SECTION 39: In cases of rape, the court shall presume absence of consent when the victim states in her evidence that she did not consent.

SECTION 122: Communications during marriage are privileged. No person who is or has been married shall be compelled to disclose any communication made to him during marriage by any person to whom he is or has been married. However, this does not apply in proceedings against either party for an offence committed against the partner.

SECTION 137: Character of rape victim is irrelevant. In a prosecution for rape, prior sexual history of the victim is completely irrelevant and inadmissible as evidence.

SECTION 63: Electronic records including WhatsApp messages, emails, CCTV footage, call recordings are admissible as evidence if accompanied by a certificate under this section.


*** ADDITIONAL SUPREME COURT JUDGMENTS ON WOMEN'S RIGHTS ***

Domestic Violence:
Hiral P. Harsora v. Kusum Narottamdas (2016): PWDVA protections extend to women against women (mother-in-law can also be a respondent).

Rajesh Sharma v. State of UP (2017): Supreme Court issued guidelines to prevent misuse of Section 498A while maintaining protections.

Maintenance Rights:
Rajnesh v. Neha (2020): Supreme Court issued comprehensive guidelines on maintenance including: maintenance claims should be decided within 6 months, interim maintenance must be granted within 60 days, maintenance must cover rent, food, clothing, education, and medical expenses.

Varun Sreenivas v. Suchismita Acharya (2023): The educational qualification and earning capacity of the wife can be considered, but the wife's right to maintenance cannot be denied merely because she is educated.

Property Rights:
Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma (2020): Daughters' coparcenary rights under Hindu Succession Act are by birth and do not depend on whether the father was alive on September 9, 2005.

Sexual Violence:
Tukaram v. State of Maharashtra (Mathura Rape Case) (1979): Led to amendment of rape laws. Showed that absence of physical resistance does not mean consent.

State of Punjab v. Gurmit Singh (1996): Identity of rape victim should not be disclosed. The victim should not be cross-examined to prove her character. Delay in filing FIR does not discredit the victim's account.

Workplace Rights:
Air India v. Nergesh Meerza (1981): Air India's rule terminating services of air hostesses on pregnancy was held discriminatory and struck down.

Municipal Corporation of Delhi v. Female Workers (2000): Maternity benefits cannot be denied to casual, daily-wage, or contractual women workers.

Marriage and Divorce:
Naveen Kohli v. Neelu Kohli (2006): When the marriage is irretrievably broken down, divorce should be granted. Both parties should not be forced to live in an unhappy marriage.

Roxann Sharma v. Arun Sharma (2015): The mother has as much right to the custody of children as the father. The court must consider the welfare of the child, not the rights of the parents.

Personal Liberty:
Hadiya case - Shafin Jahan v. Asokan K.M. (2018): An adult woman's right to choose her partner, religion, and way of life cannot be curtailed by parents, community, or the state.

Right to Privacy:
Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017): Right to privacy is a fundamental right. This protects women's reproductive choices, sexual orientation, and personal autonomy.


*** IMPORTANT GOVERNMENT SCHEMES FOR WOMEN ***

Beti Bachao Beti Padhao: Aims to prevent gender-biased sex selection, ensure survival and protection of girl child, and promote education of girls.

One Stop Centre (Sakhi): Provides integrated support services (medical, legal, psychological, shelter) to women affected by violence, in a single location. Available in every district.

Mahila Police Volunteer: Community volunteers attached to police stations to report violence and assist women in distress.

Women Helpline (181): 24x7 toll-free helpline providing immediate assistance and referral services to women in distress.

Ujjawala: Scheme for prevention, rescue, and rehabilitation of victims of trafficking for commercial sexual exploitation.

Swadhar Greh: Provides shelter, food, clothing, medical care, and legal aid to women in difficult circumstances including domestic violence survivors, women prisoners, women victims of trafficking, and single women.

Mahila Shakti Kendra: Provides support services to rural women through community engagement, awareness generation, and skill development.

Working Women Hostel: Safe and affordable accommodation for working women in urban, semi-urban, or rural areas.

National Commission for Women (NCW): Statutory body that reviews safeguards for women, investigates complaints, and recommends legislation. Contact: 7827-170-170 (WhatsApp), ncw.nic.in.

State Women Commissions: Each state has its own State Women Commission to address state-specific issues and complaints.

NALSA (Legal Aid): Free legal services available to all women regardless of income. Helpline: 15100.
