
*** DETAILED PROCEDURES: HOW TO FILE COMPLAINTS ***


*** PROCEDURE: HOW TO FILE A DOMESTIC VIOLENCE COMPLAINT ***

Step 1: Go to the nearest police station (any police station will accept - Zero FIR applies).
Step 2: Tell the police officer you want to file a Domestic Incident Report (DIR) under the PWDVA 2005.
Step 3: The police officer MUST help you. They cannot refuse. If they refuse, write to the Superintendent of Police.
Step 4: You can also go directly to the Magistrate Court without going to the police. File an application under Section 12 of PWDVA.
Step 5: You can approach the Protection Officer of your area. Ask the police or call 181 to find your area's Protection Officer.
Step 6: Documents to carry: Your ID (Aadhaar, voter ID, any ID), marriage certificate if available, photos of injuries if any, medical reports if any. If you don't have documents, you can still file — the Protection Officer will help.
Step 7: You can get an ex-parte order (emergency order without hearing the other side) if there is immediate danger.
Step 8: The Magistrate must give first hearing within 3 days and decide the case within 60 days.
Step 9: You are entitled to FREE legal aid. A free government lawyer will represent you. Call NALSA at 15100.
Step 10: The court can give you: Protection Order (stops violence), Residence Order (you keep the house), Monetary Relief (monthly money), Custody of Children, and Compensation.


*** PROCEDURE: HOW TO FILE A SEXUAL HARASSMENT COMPLAINT AT WORKPLACE (POSH) ***

Step 1: Write a complaint describing the incidents. Include dates, times, places, and names of witnesses if any.
Step 2: Submit the written complaint to the Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) of your organization.
Step 3: If your organization has fewer than 10 employees or the harasser is the employer himself, file with the Local Complaints Committee (LCC) at the District Collector's office.
Step 4: Time limit: You must file within 3 months of the incident. This can be extended by another 3 months if the ICC is satisfied with the reasons for delay.
Step 5: You can also request: transfer of yourself or the respondent, leave of up to 3 months (in addition to regular leave), and restraining the respondent from evaluating your work.
Step 6: The ICC must complete inquiry within 90 days.
Step 7: You can file a complaint on She-Box portal: shebox.nic.in (online, from anywhere).
Step 8: If ICC doesn't exist in your workplace, the employer is liable for a fine of Rs. 50,000.
Step 9: You can simultaneously file a police complaint under BNS Section 75.


*** PROCEDURE: HOW TO FILE A RAPE OR SEXUAL ASSAULT COMPLAINT ***

Step 1: Go to any police station. You can file a Zero FIR at ANY police station — they cannot refuse or redirect you.
Step 2: You have the right to file the complaint with a WOMAN police officer.
Step 3: Your statement must be recorded in the presence of a woman police officer or woman magistrate, at a place of your choice (including your home).
Step 4: The police MUST register an FIR. If they refuse, you can: (a) contact the Superintendent of Police, (b) go directly to the Magistrate under BNSS Section 175, (c) file online complaint at ncw.nic.in.
Step 5: Go to a government hospital for a medical examination. You have the right to be examined by a WOMAN doctor. The examination and MLC (Medico-Legal Certificate) are FREE.
Step 6: The two-finger test is BANNED by the Supreme Court. If any doctor tries to perform it, refuse and report them.
Step 7: You are entitled to FREE legal aid. Call NALSA at 15100.
Step 8: Your identity is legally protected. No one (police, media, courts) can reveal your name or identity. Violation is punishable.
Step 9: Your past sexual history is IRRELEVANT and cannot be used as evidence. Your character cannot be questioned in court.
Step 10: If injured, you are entitled to free medical treatment at any hospital. The hospital cannot refuse.
Step 11: DNA and forensic evidence is collected during medical examination. Try to preserve evidence — do not wash, bathe, or change clothes before the examination if possible.
Step 12: Trial must happen in a Special Fast Track Court. The case should be completed within 2 months.
Step 13: Victim compensation is available from the State Victim Compensation Fund. Apply through your lawyer or the police.


*** PROCEDURE: HOW TO FILE A DOWRY COMPLAINT ***

Step 1: File an FIR at any police station under: (a) BNS Section 85 (cruelty by husband or relatives), (b) Dowry Prohibition Act Sections 3 and 4.
Step 2: You can also file a complaint directly to the Magistrate under BNSS Section 175.
Step 3: Evidence to collect: WhatsApp messages, call recordings, emails, text messages showing dowry demands. Gift receipts, bank transaction records showing money given as dowry. Witness statements from relatives, friends, or neighbors.
Step 4: You can file a case under PWDVA simultaneously to get immediate protection, housing, and monthly maintenance.
Step 5: If dowry-related cruelty leads to the woman's death within 7 years of marriage, it is treated as "Dowry Death" under BNS Section 86, with a minimum punishment of 7 years extending to life imprisonment.
Step 6: The burden of proof is on the accused to show that dowry was not demanded (Section 8A, Dowry Prohibition Act).


*** PROCEDURE: HOW TO GET MAINTENANCE (MONTHLY SUPPORT MONEY) ***

Step 1: You can claim maintenance under THREE different laws:
(a) BNSS Section 144 (formerly CrPC 125) — applies to ALL women regardless of religion. File in Magistrate Court.
(b) PWDVA Section 20 — along with domestic violence case. File in Magistrate Court.
(c) Hindu Marriage Act Section 24/25 — during or after divorce. File in Family Court.

Step 2: For BNSS 144 maintenance: Go to the nearest Magistrate Court. You can file yourself or through a lawyer (free lawyers available through NALSA). The court MUST decide within 60 days.

Step 3: Maintenance amount: The court considers: husband's income, wife's income (if any), number of children, standard of living, medical needs, rent, and cost of education for children.

Step 4: Interim (temporary) maintenance: You can get maintenance even during the case. The court must grant interim maintenance within 60 days of the first hearing.

Step 5: Maintenance is payable for: wife, children (legitimate and illegitimate), and elderly parents.

Step 6: If the husband does not pay the ordered maintenance, the court can order attachment of his salary, bank accounts, or property. He can also be imprisoned for up to 1 month for each default.

Step 7: Supreme Court guidelines (Rajnesh v. Neha, 2020): Both parties must file affidavits disclosing their income, assets, and expenses. The court should decide maintenance applications within 6 months.


*** PROCEDURE: HOW TO REPORT CYBER CRIMES AGAINST WOMEN ***

Step 1: Go to cybercrime.gov.in and register a complaint. Select "Women/Child Related Crime" category.
Step 2: Alternatively, call the National Cyber Crime Helpline: 1930.
Step 3: You can also file an FIR at any police station. Cyber cells exist in every district.
Step 4: Preserve evidence: Take screenshots of messages, posts, images BEFORE they are deleted. Note URLs, usernames, phone numbers, and email addresses used by the offender.
Step 5: If someone is sharing your intimate images without consent (revenge porn): File under IT Act Section 66E and BNS Section 77. Request immediate takedown order from the court.
Step 6: If you are being stalked online: File under IT Act Section 66D and BNS Section 78.
Step 7: If someone created a fake profile using your identity: File under IT Act Section 66C.
Step 8: Report platforms directly: Most social media platforms have reporting mechanisms. Use them simultaneously with police complaints.
Step 9: Contact NCW WhatsApp helpline: 7827-170-170 for additional assistance.


*** PROCEDURE: HOW TO GET A PROTECTION ORDER ***

Step 1: File an application under PWDVA Section 18 in Magistrate Court.
Step 2: You can apply through: a Protection Officer, a lawyer, a service provider (NGO), or directly.
Step 3: The court can issue an ex-parte order (without hearing the other side) if there is immediate danger.
Step 4: A Protection Order can prohibit the abuser from:
(a) Committing any act of domestic violence
(b) Entering your home, workplace, or school
(c) Contacting you by phone, email, or any means
(d) Alienating any assets (selling property, emptying bank accounts)
(e) Operating bank accounts or stridhan held jointly
Step 5: Violation of a Protection Order is a criminal offence: imprisonment up to 1 year, fine up to Rs. 20,000, or both.
Step 6: The order remains valid until the court modifies or revokes it.
Step 7: If the abuser violates the order, immediately approach the police. They MUST arrest the violator without warrant.


*** PROCEDURE: HOW TO GET DIVORCE ***

For Hindu, Buddhist, Sikh, Jain women (Hindu Marriage Act, 1955):
Step 1: File a petition in Family Court by engaging a lawyer (free through NALSA if needed).
Step 2: Grounds for divorce: cruelty, desertion (2+ years), adultery, conversion, unsoundness of mind, venereal disease, renunciation, presumed death (7+ years not heard).
Step 3: Mutual consent divorce (Section 13B): Living separately for 1+ year, both agree. File joint petition. Court may waive the 6-month cooling period (Supreme Court ruling in Amardeep Singh, 2017).
Step 4: Ask for: maintenance (Section 25), child custody (Section 26), property settlement (Section 27).

For Muslim women:
Step 1: You can seek Khula (divorce initiated by wife) through Family Court under Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act, 1939.
Step 2: Grounds include: husband's cruelty, failure to maintain, imprisonment of 7+ years, impotence, insanity, forced marriage before 15 (repudiated before 18).
Step 3: Triple talaq (instant divorce by husband) is ILLEGAL and a criminal offence (Muslim Women Act, 2019). If your husband gives triple talaq, you can file a criminal complaint.

For Christian women (Indian Divorce Act, 1869):
Step 1: File in District Court.
Step 2: Grounds: adultery, cruelty, desertion (2+ years), conversion, unsoundness of mind, presumed death.

For women married under Special Marriage Act:
Step 1: File in District Court.
Step 2: Same grounds as Hindu Marriage Act.


*** RIGHTS OF WOMEN IN LIVE-IN RELATIONSHIPS ***

Supreme Court decisions have established:
1. A woman in a long-term live-in relationship has the right to maintenance under BNSS Section 144 (formerly CrPC 125) - Chanmuniya v. Chanmuniya Kumar Singh (2011).
2. PWDVA 2005 applies to women in live-in relationships. They can seek protection orders, maintenance, and compensation.
3. Children born in live-in relationships are legitimate and have inheritance rights - S.P.S. Balasubramanyam v. Suruttayan (1994).
4. A woman who has been in a live-in relationship for a significant period is entitled to maintenance - D. Velusamy v. D. Patchaiammal (2010).


*** RIGHTS OF WIDOWS ***

1. A Hindu widow has full ownership of any property she possessed during marriage (Hindu Succession Act, Section 14).
2. A widow has equal share in her deceased husband's property along with children (Hindu Succession Act, Section 15).
3. A widow is entitled to maintenance from the joint family property (Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, Section 19).
4. Widow remarriage is legally valid (Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act, 1856).
5. A widow can adopt a child under the Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act.
6. Widow pension: All states provide monthly pension to widows. Apply at the District Social Welfare Office or online at the respective state portal.
7. National Family Benefit Scheme: Rs. 20,000 lump sum assistance if the primary breadwinner (husband) dies. Applied through the BDO office.
8. Indira Gandhi National Widow Pension: Rs. 300 per month for BPL widows. After age 80, this increases to Rs. 500.


*** RIGHTS OF SINGLE AND UNMARRIED WOMEN ***

1. Single women have all the same legal rights as married women regarding property, employment, and fundamental rights.
2. Unmarried women can terminate pregnancy up to 24 weeks under MTP Act 2021 (the 2021 amendment removed the requirement of being married).
3. Single mothers can get a birth certificate for their child without naming the father.
4. Single women can adopt a child under the Juvenile Justice Act and Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act.
5. The National Food Security Act treats single women as separate households for the purpose of ration cards.
6. Working women's hostels are available for single working women in cities (Swadhar Greh scheme).


*** RIGHTS OF WOMEN DURING ARREST AND IN POLICE CUSTODY ***

1. A woman cannot be arrested after sunset and before sunrise except in exceptional circumstances, and only by a woman police officer with prior permission of a Judicial Magistrate (BNSS Section 193).
2. A woman must be searched only by another woman, with strict regard to decency (BNSS Section 53).
3. A woman arrested must be kept in a separate lock-up from men.
4. A woman in police custody has the right to free legal aid and to contact her family immediately.
5. A woman prisoner shall be kept under the care of women warders.
6. Pregnant women prisoners are entitled to pre-natal and post-natal care.
7. Women prisoners can keep their children with them in prison until the child is 6 years old.
8. No handcuffs shall be used on women prisoners except in extraordinary circumstances.


*** RIGHTS OF WOMEN IN PROPERTY AND INHERITANCE ***

Hindu Women:
1. Daughters have equal coparcenary rights as sons in the family's ancestral (joint family) property (Hindu Succession Act, 2005 amendment).
2. A woman has full absolute ownership of her stridhan (gifts and property given to her before, during or after marriage).
3. A married woman can hold, acquire, and dispose of property in her own name.
4. A Hindu woman can inherit from both her father's and husband's families.

Muslim Women:
1. A Muslim woman inherits a specific share from her parents: if she has brothers, she gets half the share of a brother. If no brothers, she gets two-thirds of the estate.
2. Mahr (dower) is the obligatory payment from husband to wife at the time of marriage. It is the wife's absolute property.
3. Muslim women have the right to maintenance during the iddat period (3 months after divorce).
4. Under the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986 and Supreme Court decisions, Muslim women can claim maintenance under BNSS Section 144.

Christian Women:
1. Indian Succession Act, 1925 applies. A widow gets one-third of the husband's estate if there are children, and the whole estate if no children.

All Women:
1. No court can force a woman to vacate her matrimonial home while a domestic violence case is pending.
2. Stridhan (wedding gifts, jewelry, etc.) is the absolute property of the woman. Taking it is a crime.
3. A working woman's salary and savings are her own property — they cannot be claimed by the husband.
